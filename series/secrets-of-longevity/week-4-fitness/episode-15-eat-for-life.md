# EPISODE 15
## Eat for Life

> **Series:** Secrets of Longevity — Week 4: Fitness (*Stewarding the Body*)

### Key Scripture
> "And God said, Behold, I have given you every herb bearing seed, which is upon the face
> of all the earth, and every tree, in the which is the fruit of a tree yielding seed; to
> you it shall be for meat."
> — Genesis 1:29 (KJV)

---

### Hook
Three times a day, you make a decision most people never think twice about.

You decide what to eat.

A small choice — a fork, a plate, a snack.

But multiply it.

Three meals a day.

A thousand meals a year.

Tens of thousands of choices over a lifetime.

Few habits shape your body, your energy, and your years more quietly —

and more powerfully —

than what you do at the table.

The table is not just where you feed your body.

It is where you practice stewardship —

or surrender it.

---

### Big Idea
> What you eat is a matter of stewardship, and discipline at the table is an act of worship.

---

### Why This Matters
The very first test in the Bible was about food.

"Of every tree of the garden thou mayest freely eat: but of the tree of the knowledge of
good and evil, thou shalt not eat" (Genesis 2:16–17).

Eden's first command involved the table.

And it still does.

How we handle appetite reveals whether we are mastering ourselves —

or being mastered.

---

### Teaching

#### 1. God Designed Food for Life, Not Just Pleasure
From the beginning, God gave food as a gift — fruit, grain, every herb of the field.

Food was His provision for life and strength.

It was never meant to be an enemy, and never meant to be an idol.

We can be thankful for food *and* wise about it. Both honor the Giver.

**Application:** Receive food with thanksgiving as God's good gift — and let gratitude make
you wise, not careless.

**Supporting Scriptures:** Genesis 1:29; Psalm 104:14–15; 1 Timothy 4:4

#### 2. Discipline at the Table Is Stewardship
The body God gave you is fueled by what you feed it.

Good fuel strengthens you for service; poor fuel slowly weakens the temple.

Stewardship doesn't mean fear or fad diets.

It means thoughtful, prayerful care — choosing what sustains the life God gave you for His
purposes.

**Application:** Make one wiser choice at your next meal — more of what gives life, less of
what only gives a moment's pleasure.

**Supporting Scriptures:** Proverbs 23:2; 1 Corinthians 9:25–27; Daniel 1:12–15

#### 3. Moderation Is God's Wisdom
"Hast thou found honey? eat so much as is sufficient for thee, lest thou be filled
therewith, and vomit it" (Proverbs 25:16).

Even honey — sweet, good, God-given — can be eaten to excess.

The issue is rarely whether a thing is good. The issue is *how much*.

"Put a knife to thy throat, if thou be a man given to appetite" (Proverbs 23:2) — a vivid
warning against being ruled by craving.

**Application:** Practice "enough." Stop before you are stuffed. Let moderation, not
appetite, hold the fork.

**Supporting Scriptures:** Proverbs 25:16; Proverbs 23:2; Proverbs 23:20–21

#### 4. The Heart Behind the Plate
Gluttony is named among the works of the flesh because it is, at root, a heart that lives
to satisfy itself.

But "whether therefore ye eat, or drink... do all to the glory of God" (1 Corinthians 10:31).

The goal is not legalism. It is a heart so satisfied in God that the body no longer rules.

**Application:** Before you eat, pause and pray. Let God, not hunger, be the master of the
moment.

**Supporting Scriptures:** 1 Corinthians 10:31; Philippians 3:19; Galatians 5:23

---

### Biblical Example
**Daniel** stands as the great picture of stewardship at the table.

Carried into Babylon, he was offered the king's rich food and wine — the best the empire
could give.

But "Daniel purposed in his heart that he would not defile himself with the portion of the
king's meat" (Daniel 1:8).

He asked instead for pulse (vegetables) and water, just for a test of ten days.

And at the end?

"Their countenances appeared fairer and fatter in flesh than all the children which did eat
the portion of the king's meat" (Daniel 1:15).

God honored his discipline. Daniel served kings for the next seventy years.

His longevity began with a decision at the table.

---

### Illustration
Think of an athlete in training for the Olympics.

She does not eat whatever happens to be in front of her.

She eats for the race.

Every meal is measured against one question: *Will this help me run?*

She isn't joyless — she simply has a goal bigger than the moment's craving.

Paul saw it: "Every man that striveth for the mastery is temperate in all things. Now they
do it to obtain a corruptible crown; but we an incorruptible" (1 Corinthians 9:25).

You are in training too —

not for a medal that fades,

but for a life that finishes the course God set before you.

Eat for the race.

---

### Application
**What should I do today?**

- Thank God for your food before you eat — out loud, on purpose.
- Choose one meal today and make it a *stewardship* meal: simpler, healthier, enough.
- Stop eating before you're full; practice the discipline of "sufficient."
- Ask before a craving: *"Am I feeding the temple, or feeding the flesh?"*

---

### Reflection Questions
- Does my appetite serve me, or do I serve my appetite?
- Where do I need the discipline that Daniel showed at the table?
- Am I eating for momentary pleasure, or for a long life of service to God?

### Longevity Insight
> Across the world, the places where people most often live past one hundred share a quiet
> pattern: they eat simply, they eat in moderation, and they stop before they are full.
> What modern study confirms, Scripture taught long ago — "eat so much as is sufficient for
> thee" (Proverbs 25:16). Daniel's ten-day test became a seventy-year career of service.
> Discipline at the table is not deprivation; it is one of the simplest, oldest secrets of
> a long life lived for God.
