# EPISODE 22
## Finish Your Race

> **Series:** Secrets of Longevity — Week 5: Finishing (*Leaving a Legacy*)

### Key Scripture
> "I have fought a good fight, I have finished my course, I have kept the faith."
> — 2 Timothy 4:7 (KJV)

---

### Hook
Anyone can start.

The blocks are crowded at the beginning of every race.

The hard part is not the gun.

The hard part is the last lap —

when the legs are heavy,

the crowd has thinned,

and no one would blame you for slowing down.

Heaven does not celebrate the fastest start.

Heaven celebrates the **finish.**

And the question that will matter on your final day is not *How did you begin?*

It is *Did you finish?*

---

### Big Idea
> God's goal for your life is not merely a long race, but a finished one — run with faith,
> joy, and confidence to the very end.

---

### Why This Matters
The Bible is honest: many start well and fail to finish.

Saul began as a humble king and ended consulting a witch. Demas walked with Paul, then
"forsook me, having loved this present world" (2 Timothy 4:10).

Starting is no guarantee of finishing.

But Scripture also shows us finishers — people who crossed the line with the baton still
in hand, still believing, still rejoicing. They were not superhuman. They simply refused
to quit.

You are still running. The line is still ahead. **How you finish is still being decided.**

---

### Teaching

#### 1. Finish with Faith — Keep Believing to the End
Paul's testimony has three parts, and the last is the foundation of the other two: *"I have
kept the faith."*

He did not say he kept the rules, or the reputation, or the crowd. He kept the *faith* —
his confidence in God held all the way to the executioner's block.

The race is not finished by the strongest legs but by the steadiest faith. Many things will
try to shake your trust in God before the end — disappointment, delay, suffering, the long
silence of unanswered prayer. To finish is to still be believing when you cross the line.

**Application:** Guard your faith above your comfort and your reputation. Decide now that
nothing — no loss, no delay, no offense — will make you stop trusting God before the end.

**Supporting Scriptures:** 2 Timothy 4:7; Hebrews 12:1–2; Revelation 2:10

#### 2. Finish with Strength — Don't Coast to the Line
Caleb is the picture of a man who refused to retire from the fight. At eighty-five he said,
*"Now therefore give me this mountain"* (Joshua 14:12). He didn't ask for a rocking chair;
he asked for the *hardest* assignment — a mountain full of giants.

Finishing well is not coasting. It is pressing toward the mark, straining for the prize,
*"forgetting those things which are behind"* (Philippians 3:13–14).

The temptation in the later miles is to ease off, to live on past victories. But God still
has mountains to give those willing to take them.

**Application:** Ask God for a "mountain" — a fresh assignment for this season. Refuse to
coast. Run *through* the line, not *to* it.

**Supporting Scriptures:** Joshua 14:10–14; Philippians 3:13–14; 1 Corinthians 9:24–26

#### 3. Finish with Joy — Count It a Privilege, Not a Burden
Paul faced execution, yet his letter overflows with confidence and even triumph: *"the
time of my departure is at hand"* — and in the very next breath, *"there is laid up for me
a crown"* (2 Timothy 4:6, 8).

Finishing well is not grim endurance. It is joyful expectation. Paul's eyes were on the
crown, not the chains.

We "run with patience the race that is set before us, looking unto Jesus... who for the joy
that was set before him endured the cross" (Hebrews 12:1–2). Joy is what carries a runner
through the painful last miles.

**Application:** Fix your eyes on the joy ahead — the crown, the "well done," the face of
Jesus. Let that joy, not mere willpower, carry you the rest of the way.

**Supporting Scriptures:** 2 Timothy 4:6–8; Hebrews 12:1–2; Acts 20:24

#### 4. Finish with Confidence — Know Whose Hand Holds You
The Apostle John outlived all the other apostles, finishing his race in exile on Patmos —
old, alone, but full of revelation, given the very vision of the risen Christ
(Revelation 1:9–18).

How do you finish with confidence? Not by trusting your own grip, but by trusting His.
Paul said, *"I know whom I have believed, and am persuaded that he is able to keep that
which I have committed unto him against that day"* (2 Timothy 1:12).

The finisher is confident not because he is strong, but because the One who began a good
work in him "will perform it until the day of Jesus Christ" (Philippians 1:6).

**Application:** Rest your confidence on God's faithfulness, not your own strength. He who
called you is faithful; He will carry you home.

**Supporting Scriptures:** 2 Timothy 1:12; Philippians 1:6; Jude 1:24

---

### Biblical Example
- **Caleb** finished with *strength* — at eighty-five, still asking for a mountain
  (Joshua 14:10–14). He "wholly followed the LORD" from start to finish.
- **Paul** finished with *faith and joy* — facing death he wrote, "I have finished my
  course, I have kept the faith... henceforth there is laid up for me a crown of
  righteousness" (2 Timothy 4:7–8).
- **John** finished with *confidence* — old and exiled on Patmos, he was still walking with
  God so closely that he received the Revelation of Jesus Christ (Revelation 1:9–11).

Three men, three finishes — all crossing the line still following the Lord. None of them
quit.

---

### Illustration
In the 1968 Mexico City Olympics, a marathon runner named John Stephen Akhwari of Tanzania
fell during the race and badly injured his leg.

The winner had long since crossed the line. The stadium was nearly empty. Night was
falling.

Then, more than an hour later, he limped into the stadium — bleeding, bandaged, barely able
to walk — and finished the race.

When asked why he didn't quit, he said: *"My country did not send me 5,000 miles to start
the race. They sent me to finish the race."*

**A modern picture:** God did not save you to *start* the Christian life. He saved you to
*finish* it. The crowd may thin and the legs may fail, but the finish line is what you were
sent for. **Don't quit on the last lap.**

---

### Application
**What should I do today?**

- Take inventory: where are you tempted to slow down, coast, or quit? Name it honestly.
- Ask God for a fresh "mountain" — a new assignment for this season, not just a rest from
  old ones.
- Fix one image in your mind for the hard miles ahead: the crown, the "well done," the face
  of Jesus. Run toward that.

---

### Reflection Questions
- Am I running to coast across the line, or to finish strong?
- Is my faith something I am keeping all the way to the end, or quietly setting down?

### Prayer

Let me finish with joy — my eyes on the crown and on the face of Jesus.

### Longevity Insight
> Marathon coaches know that most runners don't lose the race in the first miles — they
> lose it in the mind, in the final stretch, when the body screams and the will gives way.
> The finishers are the ones who fixed their eyes on the line and refused to listen to
> their legs. The Christian life is the ultimate distance race. The crown is not given for
> a fast start, but for a faithful finish. Keep your eyes on Jesus, and *don't stop running
> until you cross the line.*
